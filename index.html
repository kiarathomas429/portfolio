Eating While Black

Only 3 percent of Americans in general, but 8 percent of Black Americans are vegan. Whether it’s to reduce animal cruelty, manage a chronic illness, or connect with their heritage and build a social movement, Black people are embracing plant-based diets.

He Cured His Diabetes By Swearing Off Goat

In December 2019, Michael Ngigi’s father was visiting relatives in Kenya when he suddenly felt disoriented and mumbled incoherently. He was admitted to the ICU, but refused to get his vitals tested or have his blood drawn by doctors for blood tests to determine the cause of this episode, unless his sons would do the same. So they did.

Ngigi, 36, discovered that his blood sugar and blood pressure were high. He went back to the hospital the next day to visit his father and see the doctor again. He was diagnosed with type 2 diabetes. His body wasn’t producing enough insulin to regulate his sugar levels.

The news hit him especially hard, because it did not come out of the blue. Type 2 diabetes ran in his family. He had witnessed his mom deal with the chronic disease for many years. Now his father was in the ICU with extremely high sugar levels and vitals that were “off the charts.” Ngigi was scared. His first child had just been born. This was his wakeup call.

“The first thing that the doctor said was, ‘It will never go away.’ And that made me feel furious,” he said. “I couldn't understand how I didn't have a way out. And so I decided that I was going to do everything I could. I was going to sacrifice everything I could to beat it.”

Ngigi’s father was released from the hospital and Ngigi began self monitoring his glucose levels and blood pressure and keeping daily records. He read about diabetes and treatments for the disease. He sifted through the information overload and misinformation on the Internet. He tried the keto diet, a low carb high and fat diet, and noticed that his health was going downhill fast. He said his blood pressure became worse, was disoriented and had a lot of anxiety. He came across an article about how eating a plant-based whole grains diet could help reverse diabetes and decided to try it out. And. It wouldn’t be easy: he was accustomed to eating meat, especially goat, every day on his family farm. But he hoped an abundance of fruits and vegetables would be his cure.

Diabetes is on the rise, in Africa and around the world, according to the International Diabetes Federation. And this illness is hitting Black people especially hard. In the United States, where more than 11% are diagnosed with diabetes and almost 13% of them are Black. The highest of any race, according to the Centers for Disease Control. In 2018, Black Americans were twice as likely to die from diabetes as whites, according to the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services. They’re also more likely to suffer the worst consequences such as blindness and amputations. Experts say structural inequities including inadequate access to health care and discrimination create these disparities.

Black people tend to have less access to health insurance to pay for insulin and other diabetes medications, which means they dont always take it, or they skip doses, which makes their disease worse. But research suggests that diabetics may be able to control the disease, completely or in part, with something much simpler and more accessible: food. A 2021 Italian review study looked at 15 previous observational and randomized controlled studies, which examined the incidence rate of type 2 diabetes in vegans and people who ate a plant-based diet and how these diets impacted controlling glucose levels and diabetes-related complications in type 2 diabetes. The study found that people who eat a vegan diet are less likely to be diagnosed with type 2 diabetes, and that plant based foods lowered glucose levels for those who had type 2 diabetes.

“Type two diabetes used to be seen as a life sentence. Once you had it, there was no going back,” Victoria Moran, certified holistic health counselor said. “But we know now that that is not true…Choosing a plant based diet, getting regular exercise, taking care of our stress are the ways that we hedge our bets.”

Diabetes is a chronic metabolic disease that causes a person’s blood sugar to be high. This can cause hyperglycemia, or too much sugar in the blood. If left untreated this can cause damage to organs, blood vessels, tissues and nerves. Diabetes can happen when the pancreas doesn’t produce any or enough insulin, or “when the body cannot effectively use the insulin it produces,” according to the World Health Organization. Type 1 diabetes is a genetic condition where the cells in the immune system attack the cells in the pancreas causing little to no insulin to be produced. Type 2 diabetes is lifestyle-related. Insulin is needed to regulate blood sugar levels in the bloodstream by allowing the glucose to enter the cells and provide energy.

Diabetics are generally told to avoid foods that are high in saturated fat, trans fat, sodium, sweetener and sugar to lower their glucose, blood pressure and cholesterol levels, according to the National Institute of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases. Doctors recommend people who have diabetes are told to eat lean meats, chicken or turkey without the skin, and that at least half of their daily consumption of grains should be whole grains. Traditionally, type 2 diabetics are told to eat a low carb diet to decrease their blood sugar levels. But a 2021 systematic review showed that the first six months of a low carb diet worked, but afterwards the advantages decreased due to the diet being restrictive. People who ate a low carb diet had a 32% increase in diabetes remission compared to other diets.

The reason why a vegan diet is a great option is because it’s well-rounded, Whitney Miner, certified holistic nutritionist and vegan health coach said.

Foods that have simple carbohydrates, like milk products and high-fructose corn syrup, digest quickly and cause your blood sugar to spike. Fruits and vegetables have simple carbohydrates too, but they’re natural and rich in fiber. The fiber helps regulate blood sugar because it slows down digestion. This prevents a surge in blood sugar levels and is why a vegan diet is a great way to reverse type 2 diabetes, Miner said.

“Food will always be our first form of medication,” Kyhla Desire, a board member of the African American Diabetes Association, said. “There will never be a con to having a balanced diet that allows one to control their health. However, all questions of medication or healthcare plans should be addressed and discussed with a medical professional before any changes are made.”

When Ngigi was diagnosed he was over 200 pounds and stood at five feet and eight inches. He was obese, according to the National Heart, Lung and Blood Institute BMI calculator. He slimmed down to about 163 pounds after tk time of plant based eating. And at a doctors visit just under a year after his diagnosis, he learned that he had reversed his diabetes. He stopped taking his medication and continued a plant-based diet. His family members were influenced by his decision. His brother’s high blood pressure disappeared after following in Ngigi’s “foodsteps.”
